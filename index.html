The "Trump Effect"

A post-election survey about populism in the US and the EU-28

Introduction

The election of Donald Trump has taken the world by surprise and is fuelling populist movements across the Western Hemisphere. Dalia Research is a marketing consulting firm whose goal is to "change the way that attitudinal data is collected, analysed and presented" with an emphasis on quality and depth of data. Dalia Research is responsible for this data and has published them under the CC BY-NC-SA 4.0 License. Transformations to this data were made to suit the visualization techniques, and this visualization is published under the same license as the data.

Understanding a citizenry's attitude toward outsiders is a tremendous challenge. Some questions this dashboard visualization was designed to help answer are:

  • What proportion of people trust the judgment of experts?
  • What is the relationship between trust in the judgment of experts and disposable income?
  • How is the income distribution different in the country as opposed to cities?
  • What does a voter's worldview tell us about who they are likely to vote for?

Analysis

After interacting with the dashboard, several observations are possible. The bar charts reveal a preference for one's own judgment rather than an expert, and the stacked bars for Urban are somewhat dilated compared to Rural; while Rural has more of the poorest of the poor and the richest of the rich, Urban has proportionately more of it's population with between 2400£ - 9600£ of disposable income each month. The heatmap, on its face, doesn't show much- many people chose not to share how much extra money they had in addition to not showing a preference for their own judgment or that of experts', but focusing on each column reveals some structure. Several of the bars have extreme values at the top and bottom, but they each have a hot spot somewhere in the middle as well. Moving from left to right, that hot spot moves upward. The "Disagree" and "Agree" columns are very similar, but comparing their extreme partners shows hot spots at 3200 - 4800 for "Strongly Disagree" and "800 - 1600" for "Strongly Agree". It is possible that people who make more money are more likely to prefer their own opinions over the judgment of experts! Focusing on each row by mousing over the stacked bar segments is less enlightening- the values seem dominated by the overall distribution; while there may be a trend in the heatmap, it is still true that you are more likely to prefer your own opinion over an expert's regardless of how much money you make. The pie chart has a very interesting story to tell. People with a positive world view are more like to choose any option except the Republican candidate. Not only is true that those with negative world views choose an option other than the Republican candidate less often, but those with negative worldviews are greater in their majority for choosing their next leader.

Clustering of two subgroups of features was attempted, based on demographic and voting issue features respectively. The charts below show results for different numbers of clusters. What we would see if clustering worked well is a low value for 2, with a relative maximum at some interger value greater than 2. High values are desirable because the y-axis "silhouette width" is a measure of how similar each centroid is to each observation in its cluster. We do see relative maxima at 8 clusters for demographic, and 10 clusters for voting issues, but because of the categorical nature of the features used for this clustering, these are likely artifacts of overfitting and are not useful. Results did not support the classification of respondents into subgroups.
